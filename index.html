INTRODUCTION

The Lofi vinyl is calm, repetitive, and commonly associated with studying environments. Many students use music while studying as a way to improve focus, reduce stress, and make long study sessions feel personalized. According to Goltz and Sadakata (2021), students often intentionally select music based on the cognitive demands of the task they are working on, treating music as a tool to regulate attention and productivity. However, the impact of this strategy is not consistent across individuals or situations. While some learners report improved concentration when listening to background music, others experience reduced focus, especially when the sound becomes complex or distracting.

“Students use music to optimize cognitive performance” (Goltz & Sadakata, 2021).

INSTRUMENTAL VS. HIGH-INTENSITY SOUND

Classical music is often considered the most stable of these genres. Its constant patterns, slow pacing, and lack of loud music creates an environment that supports concentration. Because it does not contain words or it allows the brain to maintain focus on academic tasks without significant disruption. In contrast, EDM and metal introduce a much higher level of stimulation. These genres are characterized by strong beats, fast tempo, and intense layering. Even without lyrics, this level of intensity can make the listening experience engaging but also more distracting. Serpil (2015) suggests that when external stimulation becomes too strong, it can negatively affect academic performance by reducing the ability to sustain deep focus and retain information. This contrast shows that the absence of lyrics alone does not determine whether music is helpful for studying. Instead, the structure and intensity of sound play a large role in how effectively the brain can maintain attention. While classical music may support a calm environment, EDM and metal can push the brain into a stimulated state that may not be ideal for learning tasks requiring concentration.

“Music with lyrics competes for cognitive resources during cognitive tasks” (Gonzalez & Aiello, 2019).
